## Post 2
**Date:** 2 Days ago

You don't need digital PR. You need web design.

A pattern I keep seeing: brands come in wanting digital PR because their rankings and LLM visibility is languishing.

More links. More authority. More mentions.

But when you actually look at the site and pages they want to promote, the problem isn't authority. It's the user experience.

No supporting data. No visualizations. No proof points that make someone stop scrolling. Just a wall of generic text that doesn't convert and doesn't earn links naturally.

The highest-ROI move isn't building 25 links to a mediocre site.

It's redesigning the site and its pages so it actually deserves them.

Add proprietary data points. Build a data visualization that tells a story. Include stats that make the page feel like a resource, not a sales pitch.

A well-designed site with great UX does two things your digital PR retainer can't:

- It converts better 
- It earns links on its own

Most teams skip this because "web design" sounds like a different department's problem. But if your website reads like a brochure and you're wondering why your link building isn't moving the needle, that's your answer.

Fix the site. Then consider if you need digital PR.

You'll spend less and get more.

## Post 3
**Date:** 5 Days ago

What's actually happening with listicle content right now — from someone in the trenches:

Google has meaningfully increased SERP diversity on listicle-heavy queries. 

Where these used to be almost exclusively roundup results, we're now seeing a healthier mix of first-party product pages alongside traditional listicle content.

Here's what that means in practice:

1. Cannibalization risk has gone up. 

Companies that scaled listicle volume to capture rankings — even those with dedicated landing pages — may be seeing traffic erosion as domain diversity increases. The fix isn't to abandon the strategy. It's to audit more carefully and make sure your pages aren't competing with each other.

2. Product page SEO/GEO now matters more than ever. 

With product pages appearing more frequently in these SERPs, the investment in what we call product page mapping — building out core use cases, industry pages, and role-specific pages for your entire ICP — has become a higher-priority lever.

3. Listicles still work. 

They're just a more targeted play now. The real value today is as a supplement to your core product pages — particularly for keyword variations that don't justify a full page but make sense as focused, standalone listicle content.

The prolific, high-volume listicle approach is tightening. That doesn't mean past efforts were wasted. Those pages built snowball effects: getting mentioned in other listicles, establishing moats in LLMs, and driving compounding visibility that still pays dividends.

The strategy isn't dead. It just requires more precision.
